Financial Action Task Force (FATF)


The Financial Action Task Force (FATF) is an intergovernmental organization founded in 1989 to combat money laundering and terrorist financing. They promote policies and procedures to prevent money laundering or illegal activities by setting up International standards. This was established by G7 countries with the primary moto to develop and promote policies to combat money laundering and terrorist financing.


FATF issues international standards and recommendations, commonly known as the FATF Recommendations, which provide a framework for countries to combat money laundering, terrorist financing, and the proliferation of weapons of mass destruction.


How many countries are members of FATF and which are those countries?

The FATF has 39 member countries, these are Argentina, Australia, Austria, Belgium, Brazil, Canada, China, Denmark, Finland,France, Germany, Greece, Hong Kong, China, Iceland, India, Ireland, Italy, Japan, Luxembourg, Malaysia, Mexico, Netherlands, New Zealand, Norway, Portugal, Russia, Singapore, South Africa, South Korea, Spain, Sweden, Switzerland, Turkey, United Kingdom, United States, Saudi Arabia, United Arab Emirates, Indonesia, Israel


Are there any other parties or members?

Yes, these are not members but FATF-Style Regional Bodies (FSRBs). There are total 9 FSRBs. The 9 FSRBs have an essential role in promoting the effective implementation of the FATF Recommendations by their membership and in providing expertise and input in FATF policy-making. They are GIABA, CFATF, MONEYVAL, EAG, ESAAMLG, GAFILAT, MENAFAFT, GABAC and APG.


Do FATF maintains any list like Blacklist?

Yes, FATF maintains a list of jurisdictions that have deficiencies in their AML/CFT regimes. This list includes countries that pose a higher risk of money laundering and terrorist financing.


How many recommendations do they have?

There are total 40 recommendations.
