Foreign Account Tax Compliance Act (FATCA)


Foreign Account Tax Compliance Act (FATCA) is a U.S. law that was established in 2010. The law is established to combat tax evasion by U.S. persons holding assets and accounts abroad. Few U.S. citizens sets up offshore entity and does not report it to the U.S. Government to evade tax. So, the main goal of FATCA is to enhance transparency in the tax and combat offshore tax evasion by requiring foreign financial institutions (FFIs) to report any information about any accounts held by U.S. persons to the Internal Revenue Service (IRS).


Who are covered under this law?

It basically applies to U.S. citizens, living abroad and residents as well as noo-residents who have significant ties to the U.S. Apart from this foreign financial institutions (FFIs) are also covered as they will be the one who will have to report it to IRS.


Why foreign financial institutions (FFIs) requires to report to IRS?

If foreign financial institutions (FFIs) wants to be FATCA compliant then this is one of the requirements. Hence, they will have to report it.


What information do the foreign financial institutions (FFIs) needs to report to IRS?

FFIs needs to report details such as names, addresses, and the highest annual account balance.


How FFIs report these information to IRS.

The U.S. has entered into bilateral agreements with many countries to facilitate FATCA implementation. These agreements may allow FFIs in partner countries to report to their local tax authorities, who then share the information with the IRS.


How FFIs show that they are in compliance with FATCA?

Through GIIN. FFIs must register themselves with the IRS and obtain a Global Intermediary Identification Number (GIIN). This GIIN shows that they are in compliant with FATCA.
