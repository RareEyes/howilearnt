USA PATRIOT Act


USA PATRIOT Act (Uniting and Strengthening America by Providing Appropriate Tools Required to Intercept and Obstruct Terrorism Act) established after 9/11 attack in 2001. The primary aim of the USA PATRIOT Act is to enhance the ability of law enforcement and intelligence agencies to prevent and investigate terrorism-related activities.


This act has included 3 main provisions:

  1. Expanding the surveillance abilities of Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Act (FISA), including by tapping domestic and international phones.
  2. Facilitating enhanced communication among federal agencies enables a more effective utilization of all accessible resources in counterterrorism endeavors.
  3. The legislation introduces heightened penalties for terrorism-related offenses and broadens the scope of activities that would warrant charges related to terrorism.

Here is a concise and non-exhaustive overview of the sections of the USA PATRIOT Act:

  • Requiring the identification of customers utilizing correspondent accounts involves obtaining information comparable to that collected for domestic customers. Additionally, it includes imposing prohibitions or conditions on the opening or maintenance of correspondent or payable-through accounts in the U.S. for a foreign banking institution.
  • Introduced Special/Enhanced Due Diligence for Correspondent Accounts and Private Banking Accounts
  • No correspondent banking/dealing with Foreign Shell Banks
  • Encourages fruther cooperation from law enforcement, regulators and financial institutions to share information of those with suspicious activities
  • U.S. banks should maintain records identifying an agent for service of legal process for its correspondent accounts.
  • The Secretary of the Treasury can make rules about how financial institutions handle concentration accounts. This is to make sure these accounts aren't used to hide who the customer is, whether they own the money directly or indirectly.
  • Established minimum standard to obtain identity of the customer opening the account
  • No officer or employee of federal, state, local, tribal, or territorial governments within the U.S., should disclose any information regarding SAR to anyone
  • The financial institution must, at the very least, create internal rules, guidelines, and checks. They also need to appoint someone to make sure they follow the rules, provide training for employees, and have an independent review to test how well everything is working.
  • This change in the law updates how we define money transmitters in the Bank Secrecy Act (BSA). It makes sure that informal or underground banking systems are now considered financial institutions and have to follow the BSA rules.
  • This law makes FinCEN create a very safe network for better communication with financial institutions. It helps banks submit reports electronically under the BSA and allows FinCEN to send alerts to banks.
