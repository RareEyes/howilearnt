Sanction:


Introduction:

Sanctions refers to the steps taken to prevent and combat money laundering and terrorist financing. AML sanctions are typically governed by national and international laws and regulations. Sanctions are restrictions with political motivations, implemented to uphold international peace and security. The purpose of sanctions is to influence the political behavior of a target, exerting pressure to promote changes that contribute to restoring international peace. These measures aim to prevent the proliferation of specific risks to national security and curb the financing of criminal activities.


Who are the targets of sanctions?

  1. Individuals
  2. Organizations
  3. Countries
  4. Sometimes a particular asset as well

What are the reasons of sanctions?

Below are few reasons of sanctions:

  • Human Rights Violations: such as genocide, torture, or systematic repression of their citizens etc.

  • Nuclear Proliferation: activities related to the proliferation of weapons of mass destruction

  • National Security Concerns: Responding to aggression, military threats, or activities perceived as a threat to the security of the imposing country or its allies

  • Terrorism: Sanctions can be imposed on countries or individuals involved in supporting or sponsoring terrorism.

  • Violations of International Law: such as by annexing territory, engaging in illegal occupations etc.

  • Cybersecurity Threats

  • Non-Compliance with Agreements: Failure to comply with international agreements or treaties can lead to sanctions.

  • Trade Imbalances: sanctions may be imposed to address trade imbalances or unfair trade practices.

  • Environmental Concerns: Countries that contribute significantly to environmental degradation or fail to address environmental concerns

  • Public Health Crises: If country's actions contribute to the spread of diseases or impede international efforts to address health emergencies.

Are there any types of sanctions?

Yes, there are many types of sanctions. Lets discuss few here:

  • Economic Sanctions:
    • Trade Embargoes: Prohibtion of trade with targeted country. Example: Trade Embargo on Cuba by United States
    • Asset Freeze: Freezing of assets owned or controlled by targeted individuals, entities, or governments. Example: Following the annexation of Crimea by Russia in 2014, the United States, the European Union, and other countries imposed asset freezes on certain Russian individuals and entities believed to be involved in the actions.
    • Financial Restriction: Imposition of limitations on financial transactions, investments, or access to international financial institutions.

  • Travel Bans: Restrictions on the travel of individuals associated with the targeted government or entities. Example: The European Union imposed travel bans on individuals associated with the Belarusian government following the controversial 2020 presidential election and subsequent crackdown on protests.

  • Arms Embargoes: Prohibition or restriction on the sale, transfer, or export of arms and military-related equipment to a targeted country or entity. Example: The United Nations Security Council imposed an arms embargo on North Korea, restricting the sale, transfer, or export of arms and related materiel to North Korea in response to its nuclear weapons program.

  • Sectoral Sanctions: Such as energy, finance, or technology,

  • Diplomatic Sanctions: Example, In response to alleged cyber-attacks and interference in democratic processes, the United States has expelled Russian diplomats and imposed other diplomatic sanctions on Russia.
    • Expulsion of Diplomats: Removal of diplomatic personnel from the targeted country.
    • Suspension of Diplomatic Relations: Cutting or suspending diplomatic ties with the targeted government.
