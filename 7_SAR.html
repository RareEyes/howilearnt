Suspicious Activity Report (SAR)


Introduction:


A Suspicious Activity Report (SAR), sometimes also termed as Suspicious Transaction Report (STR) is a tool created by the Bank Secrecy Act (BSA) in 1970. It helps keep an eye on activities that might seem strange or not get noticed in other reports, like the one about currency transactions. SARs are part of AML, wherein a document is filed by financial institutions when they suspect unusual or suspicious activity involving a customer's account. Filing SARs is a legal requirement for financial institutions to help detect and prevent financial crimes.


Who is responsible for reporting SAR?

Can anyone from financial institution report SAR? The answer is No. Then who can report it. It's the MLRO (Money Laundering Reporting Officer).


Can MLRO report any activity in SAR?

No, only those that are found suspicious and cannot be reported under Currency Transaction Report (CTR) or any other report.


What is considered to be suspicious for any financial institution to report in a SAR??

Below are few examples of when financial institution can report SAR:


  • Large Cash Transactions: Large cash transactions are generally not considered suspicious. However, if a customer fails to declare such transactions during the initial onboarding process and engages in frequent large cash transactions afterward, it is deemed suspicious.
  • Jurisdictions: Financial institutions deem certain jurisdictions to be high risk. Any transaction to or from such countries is regarded as suspicious.
  • Unusual Transactions: Transactions that deviate from a customer's typical behavior may raise concerns. For instance, if a customer typically receives small amounts of cash from a few known individuals or entities, but suddenly begins receiving either substantial amounts from familiar sources or smaller amounts from unknown customers, such activity is considered a suspicious transaction.
  • Movement of funds: Rapid movement of funds between the accounts either within the country or across border.
  • Smurfing: Deliberately breaking down the large amount into small amount of transaction to avoid reporting thresholds. Also, known as structuring.
  • Complex Transaction: When customer create layers of transaction or involve multiple parties to hide the source of funds.

To whom or which authority should the MLRO (Money Laundering Reporting Officer) report the SAR (Suspicious Activity Report)?

Generally, Financial Intelligence Unit (FIU) of the country. Every jurisdiction will have their own regulatory authority/FIU to file the file. Below are examples of few countries:


  • United States - Financial Crimes Enforcement Network (FinCEN)
  • United Kingdom - National Crime Agency (NCA)
  • Canada - Financial Transactions and Reports Analysis Centre of Canada (FINTRAC)
  • Germany - Central Unit for Financial Transaction Investigation (Zentralstelle f√ºr Finanztransaktionsuntersuchungen - FIU Germany)
  • United Arab Emirates - Financial Intelligence Unit (FIU) of the UAE
  • France - Tracfin (Traitement du renseignement et action contre les circuits financiers clandestins)
  • South Africa - Financial Intelligence Centre (FIC)
  • India - Financial Intelligence Unit - India (FIU-IND)
  • Japan - Japan Financial Intelligence Center (JAFIC)
  • Australia - Australian Transaction Reports and Analysis Centre (AUSTRAC)

Is there any timeframe within which financial insitution should report the suspicious activity?

Yes, they should report it within 30 days from the occurence of the transaction(s). An extension of no more than 60 days may be obtained, if necessary to collect more evidence.


What constitute part of Suspicious Activity Report form?


  • Financial Institution Information
    • Name of the Financial Insitution
    • Communication Address of the FI
    • MLRO Name

  • Point of Contact - Financial Institution
    • Name
    • Contact Number
    • Email ID

  • Customer Information
    • Name of the customer
    • Communication address of the customer
    • Date of Birth/Date of establishment

  • Transaction Details
    • Date of the transaction
    • Amount
    • Account number
    • Type of transaction
    • Recipient, if any

  • Nature of Suspicion
    • This section should be as detailed as possible for FIUs of the respective country to take further action.
