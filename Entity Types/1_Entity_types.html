Legal Entity Types:


There are various types under which an entity can be formed, depending on the requirements of individuals or groups. In this discussion, we will provide a brief overview of these entities here, with detailed information provided in individual sections.


  1. Private Limited Companies/Limited Liability Company
    • A private limited company is a company established by a few individuals privately. The shareholders of a private limited company cannot trade their shares publicly. A private limited company cannot issue a prospectus inviting the public to subscribe to its shares.
    • In legal terms, Section 2 (68) of the Companies Act, 2013 defines a private company as: “A Company having a minimum paid-up share capital as may be prescribed, and which by its articles,— (i) restricts the right to transfer its shares; (ii) except in case of One Person Company, limits the number of its members to two hundred; (iii) prohibits any invitation to the public to subscribe for any securities of the company.”

  2. Public Listed Companies
    • A public listed company, also known as a publicly traded company or a publicly held company, is a company whose ownership is structured in a way that allows its shares to be freely traded on a stock exchange or over-the-counter (OTC) market. These shares are bought and sold by investors in the open market.
    • A public company is a corporation whose shareholders have a claim to part of the company's assets and profits.
    • Ownership of a public company is distributed among general public shareholders through the free trade of shares of stock on stock exchanges or over-the-counter (OTC) markets.

  3. Limited Liability Partnership
    • Limited liability partnership (LLP) is a type of general partnership where every partner has a limited personal liability for the debts of the partnership.
    • LLPs allow limited liability even if partners remain involved in the management of the business.
    • For example, Mr A and Mr B have opened a restaturant and due to some reason people fell sick because of this resturant food, then they cannot both be personally sued for damages as their liability will be limited.

  4. General Partnership
    • A general partnership is a business arrangement by which two or more individuals agree to share responsibilities, assets, profits, and financial and legal liabilities of a jointly-owned business.
    • A general partnership is a form of business organization where two or more individuals manage and operate a business in accordance with the terms and objectives set out in a Partnership Deed. Each partner is personally liable for the debts and obligations of the business, and their personal assets may be used to satisfy business debts.

  5. Limited Partnership
    • A limited partnership (LP) is a form of business structure that consists of at least one general partner and one or more limited partners.
    • The general partner runs the business and has unlimited liability. Limited partners have limited liability, meaning their personal assets are generally protected from business debts and liabilities.

  6. Sole Traders
    • A sole trader, also known as a sole proprietor or sole proprietorship, is a type of business structure where an individual owns and operates a business alone.
    • It is a type of entity in which there is no legal distinction between the owner and the business entity. A sole trader does not necessarily work alone and may employ other people.

  7. Government Owned Entities
    • A government-owned entity (GOE) refers to an organization or business that is fully or partially owned and operated by a government or governmental agency.
    • Government-owned entities can take different forms, such as state-owned enterprises (SOEs), government corporations, or public-sector units.

  8. Trusts
    • A trust is a legal entity that is created when one party, known as the settlor or grantor, transfers property or assets to another party, known as the trustee, for the benefit of a third party, known as the beneficiary. Trusts are commonly used for estate planning, asset protection, and charitable purposes.

  9. Foundations
    • Foundations are legal entities that are typically established for charitable, philanthropic, educational, or other socially beneficial purposes.
    • A foundation is a type of nonprofit organization or charitable trust that usually provides funding and support to other charitable organizations through grants, while also potentially participating directly in charitable activities.

  10. Financial Institutions
    • A financial institution (FI) is a company engaged in the business of dealing with financial and monetary transactions such as deposits, loans, investments, and currency exchange. Financial institutions include a broad range of business operations within the financial services sector, including banks, insurance companies, brokerage firms, and investment dealers.
    • A financial institution is a broad term that refers to any organization or establishment that provides financial services to its customers.

  11. Non-Banking Financial Institutions
    • Non-bank financial companies (NBFCs), also known as nonbank financial institutions (NBFIs), are financial institutions that offer various banking services but do not have a banking license.
    • Non-Banking Financial Institutions (NBFI) are financial institutions that provide banking services without meeting the legal definition of a bank.

  12. Law Firms & Solicitors
    • Law firms are organizations that provide legal services to individuals, businesses, or other entities.
    • The primary service rendered by a law firm is to advise clients (individuals or corporations) about their legal rights and responsibilities, and to represent clients in civil or criminal cases, business transactions, and other matters in which legal advice and other assistance are sought.

  13. Unincorporated Business
    • An unincorporated business is a type of business structure that has not been formally registered as a corporation.
    • An unincorporated business does not have a distinct legal status apart from its owners.

  14. Sovereign Wealth Funds
    • A sovereign wealth fund (SWF) is a state-owned investment pool that is composed of financial assets such as stocks, bonds, real estate, precious metals, or other financial instruments.
    • The money typically comes from a nation's budgetary surplus. When a nation has excess money, it uses a sovereign wealth fund as a way to funnel it into investments rather than simply keeping it in the central bank or channeling it back into the economy.

  15. Funds
    • A fund is a pool of money that is allocated for a specific purpose.
    • The pool of money in a fund is often invested and professionally managed in order to generate returns for its investors.
    • There are various types of funds which will discuss in details in its own section here.
