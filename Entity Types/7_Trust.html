Trust


Introduction:


A trust is a legal entity that is created when one party, known as the settlor or grantor, transfers property or assets to another party, known as the trustee, for the benefit of a third party, known as the beneficiary. Trusts are commonly used for estate planning, asset protection, and charitable purposes.


A trust is a legal entity with separate and distinct rights, similar to a person or corporation. Generally, there will be 3 parties involved, settlor, gives another party, the trustee, the right to hold title to and manage property or assets. Trustee does this for another party known as beneficiary.


Different categories of trust:

Let's discuss few categories/trypes of trust:


  1. Living Trust: A living trust, also known as a revocable living trust or inter vivos trust, is a legal arrangement that individuals create during their lifetime to manage and distribute their assets.

    This type of trust is flexible as the settlor, grantor or trustor who sets up the trust can amend or revoke the trust during their lifetime.

    Another important point to note here is the settlor, grantor or trustor can themselves be trustee of the trust.

    The trust becomes revocable upon the death of the settlor.


  2. Testamentary Trust: Unlike a living trust, which is created during the settlor's lifetime, a testamentary trust is a post-mortem trust.

    The testamentary trust only comes into existence upon the death of the testator. (A testator is a person who makes a will).

    This type of trust is typically irrevocable and settlor cannot be a trustee, instead trustee(s) will be appointed to administer the trust.


  3. Irrevocable Trust: An irrevocable trust is a type of trust that, once established, generally cannot be altered, amended, or revoked by the person who created it (the grantor or settlor).

    Irrevocable trusts are often used for asset protection purposes. By placing assets in an irrevocable trust, they may be shielded from creditors and legal judgments.


  4. Funded Trust: A funded trust refers to a trust that has been properly and completely established by transferring assets into it.

    A trust fund, is comprised of different assets such as stocks, cash, bonds, and properties to name a few.

    The grantor can be trustee of the trust, if he/she wishes to.

    It can be revocable or irrevocable both, based on the trust deed.


  5. Unfunded Trust: An unfunded trust is a trust that has been created but has not yet had assets transferred into it.

    In simple words, the skeleton for the trust has been established, no property or assets have been formally placed under the trust's control.

    As there are no assets transferred yet, there is no role of trustee to manage any asset.


  6. Charitable Trust: A charitable trust is a type of irrevocable trust established for charitable purposes. Unlike other types of trusts, the primary beneficiary of a charitable trust is a charitable organization or cause.


    Charitable Remainder Trust (CRT): Provides income to the donor or other beneficiaries for a specified period (often the donor's lifetime) before the remaining assets go to the charitable organization.


    Charitable Lead Trust (CLT): Provides income to a charitable organization for a specified period before the remaining assets go to non-charitable beneficiaries, such as family members.
